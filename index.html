Project Objective

Tourism in Spain is strongly seasonal. Winter can favor mountain and ski destinations, summer usually strengthens coastal destinations, and major events such as Feria de Abril in Seville can create temporary peaks in demand.

This project uses accommodation data to detect these monthly opportunity patterns. It does not model each event or seasonal driver individually, but it helps identify when and where tourism revenue potential becomes strongest.

The core question is: which Spanish destination maximizes tourism opportunity in each month of the year?

The tool is intended for tourism planning, revenue management, seasonal investment screening and destination benchmarking, showing how tourism opportunity rotates across Spain throughout the calendar year.

Data Scope

The project uses INE tourism data as its primary data source. This is important because the model is built on official Spanish accommodation indicators rather than scraped listings or manually estimated demand signals.

The analysis focuses on 2023-2025. Earlier years are excluded to reduce COVID-period distortion and avoid comparing current seasonality patterns with an exceptional disruption in tourism demand.

Methodology

Hotels and tourist apartments do not provide equivalent revenue metrics in the dataset, so the model separates hotel, apartment and combined layers before creating the final weighted index.

Hotel monthly yield index RevPAR x days in month

If destination RevPAR is missing, the proxy uses autonomous community ADR multiplied by destination hotel occupancy.

Apartment opportunity index (IPAP / 100) x occupancy x days

An indexed proxy because destination-level apartment ADR or RevPAR is not available.

For tourist apartments, the project uses the national IPAP “Total tarifa” series as the general apartment price index.

Weighted tourism index Hotel monthly yield index + apartment opportunity index

The model first calculates the hotel monthly yield index and the apartment opportunity index as two separate signals. Then both indices are normalized to a 0-100 score within each month so they can be compared and combined. The final All view combines those normalized scores using available bed places as weights.

Interpretation of the Index

The key point when using this tool is that the index does not measure how many euros a destination generates. Instead, it identifies which destinations show the strongest relative tourism opportunity in each month, based on the available signals of price, occupancy and accommodation data.

This distinction is essential: the score should be interpreted as a relative monthly opportunity index, not as an absolute revenue figure or a direct measure of profitability.

Limitations

The index is a relative opportunity score, not literal revenue in euros. Hotel data is closer to revenue intensity because it can use RevPAR, while the apartment layer is an indexed proxy based on IPAP, occupancy and days in month.

  • When hotel RevPAR is available by destination, the hotel component uses observed destination RevPAR. When RevPAR by destination is missing, the model uses a hotel RevPAR proxy based on autonomous community ADR and destination hotel occupancy, rather than excluding the destination.
  • Tourist apartment results are opportunity proxies because destination-level apartment ADR or RevPAR is not available in the dataset. IPAP is a price index, not an absolute nightly euro price.
  • The model focuses on accommodation indicators and excludes non-room revenue, operating costs, margins and profitability.
  • Coverage varies by destination and accommodation type. Some destinations may have only hotel data, only tourist apartment data or incomplete monthly KPIs, so single-segment results should be read as segment-specific opportunity signals.
  • Map colors reflect relative monthly position, not a higher absolute revenue value. For example, Salamanca appears more orange in December than in September even though its absolute combined index is slightly lower in December, because in December it ranks better compared with the other destinations available that month.

Conclusions

The tool works as a tourism seasonality and relative opportunity detector. It does not measure how many euros a destination generates. Instead, it identifies which destinations show the strongest relative tourism opportunity in each month, based on available signals of price, occupancy and accommodation data.

1. The Canary Islands act as a year-round stabilizer

Many Canary Islands destinations appear strongly across the analysis, especially in winter and off-peak months. Destinations such as Adeje, San Miguel de Abona, San Bartolomé de Tirajana, Mogán, Tías, Yaiza, Santiago del Teide and Breña Baja show that the Canary Islands can act as a more stable tourism market when many mainland or seasonal coastal destinations are weaker.

For a hotel or tourist apartment operator, this suggests that the Canary Islands may be relevant for year-round planning, cash flow stability and lower dependence on the traditional summer peak.

2. The model avoids letting destination size dominate the analysis

Because the index does not multiply by total rooms or total bed places, large cities such as Madrid or Barcelona do not automatically dominate the ranking simply because they have more accommodation supply.

This makes the tool useful for identifying destinations with strong tourism intensity, even when they are smaller in absolute size.

3. Seasonality rotates across destinations and months

The tool shows a clear monthly rotation of tourism opportunity.

In winter, the strongest signals are concentrated in the Canary Islands and selected mountain destinations. Naut Aran stands out in February, reflecting ski-season demand linked to Baqueira-Beret and the Val d’Aran.

In spring, opportunity shifts toward urban and event-driven destinations. Sevilla appears strongly in April, which is consistent with Semana Santa and Feria de abril. Barcelona also gains strength in April, May and June.

In summer, the strongest signals move toward coastal and Mediterranean destinations such as Chiclana de la Frontera, Marbella, Barbate, Vera, Sitges and other beach-oriented markets.

In September and October, the pattern becomes more mixed, with Donostia/San Sebastián, Barcelona, Aínsa-Sobrarbe, Madrid and Canary Islands destinations appearing strongly.

4. Event-driven patterns are visible in the monthly winners

The monthly winners suggest that the tool is capturing not only generic seasonality, but also destination-month patterns that may be linked to local events, festivals or seasonal attractions.

Naut Aran in February: likely linked to ski-season demand in Baqueira-Beret and the Val d’Aran.

Sevilla in April: consistent with Semana Santa and Feria de abril.

Barcelona in April, May, June and October: possibly linked to a combination of international urban demand, conferences, festivals and major events such as Primavera Sound, Sónar or MWC-related business travel.

Chiclana de la Frontera in July: consistent with summer beach demand around La Barrosa and Novo Sancti Petri, potentially reinforced by summer music events such as Concert Music Festival.

Donostia/San Sebastián in September: potentially linked to the San Sebastián International Film Festival, as well as late-summer cultural and gastronomic tourism.

Aínsa-Sobrarbe in September: consistent with late-summer and early-autumn mountain, nature and rural tourism.

Canary Islands in November and December: consistent with winter-sun tourism and year-end travel demand.

5. Smaller destinations can reveal high relative intensity

The model brings visibility to smaller destinations such as Breña Baja, Tazacorte, El Paso, Alajeró, Aínsa-Sobrarbe and Cudillero.

These places are not necessarily large tourism markets by volume, but they may show strong relative opportunity in specific months due to concentrated accommodation supply, coastal tourism, nature tourism, rural tourism or seasonal demand.

6. The tool is useful for FP&A, revenue management, strategy and treasury

From a business perspective, the tool helps teams decide where to focus attention each month: pricing, campaigns, staffing, budgeting, investment, expansion and cash flow planning.

7. The accommodation filters are important

The map should be read through its three layers: Hotels, Tourist Apartments and All.

This matters because some destinations have only hotel data, others only tourist apartment data, and others both. The combined index is useful, but it should be interpreted carefully when a destination is represented by only one accommodation type.

8. The annual winners show sustained opportunity

The Top 10 annual winners highlight destinations with a more sustained tourism opportunity signal across the year.

The strong presence of Canary Islands destinations suggests that these markets may offer more stable seasonal performance, making them especially relevant for annual planning, cash flow stability and year-round accommodation strategy.

However, this ranking should be interpreted as cumulative relative opportunity, not as absolute revenue.

Executive takeaway

The tool helps reveal the seasonal rhythm of Spanish tourism: where opportunity rises, when it peaks, and which destinations deserve closer business attention each month.

Glossary

ADRAverage daily rate. In hotels, ADR represents the average price charged per occupied room. In this project, ADR is used at autonomous community level because it is multiplied by the destination hotel occupancy rate by rooms to estimate a hotel RevPAR proxy when destination-level RevPAR is missing.
Annual winnersDestinations with the highest sum of monthly index values across the year. They indicate consistently strong performance across months.
IPAPIPAP is the Tourist Apartment Price Index published by INE; it is a chained Laspeyres price index with base year 2002, meaning that it tracks how tourist apartment prices evolve over time by linking year-to-year price changes back to a common reference point where 2002 equals 100. It is an index, not an absolute euro price, and it does not provide destination-level ADR or RevPAR. In this project, IPAP is used as a national price-pressure reference for tourist apartments and is combined with destination-level occupancy by bed places to build a relative monthly opportunity proxy.
Monthly winnersDestinations that reach the highest opportunity scores within a specific month. They are useful for identifying seasonal peaks.
NormalizationScores are normalized within each month using a min-max transformation, where 100 represents the strongest destination signal in that month and 0 represents the weakest. This allows destinations and accommodation types to be compared on a common relative scale.
Occupancy ratePercentage of available accommodation capacity that is occupied. For hotels, the model prioritizes occupancy by rooms. For tourist apartments, the model uses occupancy by bed places.
RevPARRevenue per available room. It combines room price and occupancy into one hotel performance metric. In this project, RevPAR is multiplied by days in month to create the hotel monthly revenue-intensity index.
Tourist destinationDestination-level geographic unit used in the INE tourism dataset. It can differ from broader provinces or autonomous communities.
